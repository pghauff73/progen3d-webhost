Building Research

Compact architectural heuristics for generating more believable towers, slabs, courtyards, bridges, and stepped forms.

Base, middle, top

For buildings above two storeys, organize the mass as a base, a repetitive middle, and a top. In generator terms, that usually means a wider podium or plinth, a calmer shaft, and a cap, setback, or crown that finishes the silhouette.

Upper-floor reduction

Use setbacks or slightly smaller upper plates to avoid monolithic slabs. Upper-floor reduction is especially useful for stepped towers, courtyard edges, and frame buildings that need a clearer skyline profile.

Typical floor-to-floor height

Office-style floors commonly sit around 3.6 to 4.2 m, while residential-style floors are lower. Use that as a starting range for stacked floors, then reserve taller values for podiums, lobbies, and bridge decks.

Skybridge caution

Skybridges can visually and aerodynamically tie twin masses together, but the bridge itself becomes more exposed as it climbs. Mid-height links are a safer default than putting every bridge at the roof.

Tower Use a compact footprint, a repeated shaft, and one or two silhouette changes near the top. A wider podium helps the tower read as grounded instead of needle-thin at grade.
Stepped Reduce floor plate area as height increases. Stepbacks read better when they happen in a few deliberate bands instead of at every floor, and they pair well with crowns or terrace-like transitions.
Slab Break long facades into bays, panel rhythms, or visible cores. A long bar mass benefits from a strong base and roof line so it does not read like a single extruded box.
Courtyard Keep the central void large enough to register as an intentional outdoor room. The perimeter should feel structural, but the courtyard cannot collapse into a narrow slot.
Bridge Let bridge spans read as connectors between stable masses, not as the main support system. Similar datum lines on both anchor towers make the bridge feel intentional and believable.
Frame atrium Make the outer frame legible with stronger corners or edge members, then keep the atrium void simple. Too many nested cuts weaken the reading of the primary frame.
